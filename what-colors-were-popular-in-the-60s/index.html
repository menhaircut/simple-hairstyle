What Colors Were Popular In The 60S

The 1960s were a time of great change in the world. From music to fashion, everything was evolving. One of the most notable changes was in the colors that people were wearing. In this article, we'll take a look at what colors were popular in the 60s and how you can incorporate them into your wardrobe today.

Bright and Bold Colors

One of the defining characteristics of 60s fashion was the use of bright and bold colors. Colors like yellow, orange, and hot pink were all popular during this time. These colors were often worn in combination with each other, creating a colorful and eye-catching look.

The popularity of bright colors can be attributed to the optimism and hope that characterized the 60s. People were looking for new ways to express themselves and stand out, and bright colors were a way to do just that.

Today, you can incorporate bright colors into your wardrobe in a number of ways. Try wearing a bright yellow dress, or pairing a hot pink top with a pair of denim shorts. Just be sure to balance out the bold colors with neutral accessories, like a pair of nude heels or a beige bag.

Pastels

While bright colors were certainly popular in the 60s, pastels were also a common choice. Colors like baby blue, light pink, and mint green were all popular during this time. Pastels were often worn in combination with each other, creating a soft and feminine look.

Pastels were popular in the 60s because they were seen as a departure from the bold and bright colors that had been popular in the previous decade. They were also associated with the innocence and purity that characterized the youth culture of the time.

To incorporate pastels into your wardrobe, try wearing a light pink blouse with a pair of white jeans, or a mint green skirt with a white t-shirt. Pastels look great when paired with other pastels, or with neutral colors like white or beige.

Neutrals

While bright colors and pastels were both popular in the 60s, neutrals were also a common choice. Colors like black, white, and beige were all popular during this time. Neutrals were often worn in combination with each other, creating a chic and sophisticated look.

The popularity of neutrals in the 60s can be attributed to the influence of French fashion, which was all about understated elegance and simplicity. Neutrals were also seen as a way to balance out the bold and bright colors that were popular at the time.

To incorporate neutrals into your wardrobe, try wearing a black and white striped shirt with a pair of beige pants, or a white blouse with a black skirt. Neutrals look great when paired with other neutrals, or with bright colors and pastels.

Psychedelic Patterns

One of the most iconic aspects of 60s fashion was the use of psychedelic patterns. Colors like purple, green, and yellow were often used in combination with swirling and trippy patterns.

The popularity of psychedelic patterns in the 60s can be attributed to the influence of the counterculture movement, which was all about breaking free from traditional societal norms. Psychedelic patterns were a way to express this sense of rebellion and nonconformity.

To incorporate psychedelic patterns into your wardrobe, try wearing a dress or blouse with a bold, swirling print. Just be sure to keep the rest of your outfit simple, with neutral accessories and minimal jewelry.

How to Incorporate 60s Colors into Your Wardrobe

If you're looking to incorporate 60s colors into your wardrobe, there are a few things to keep in mind. First, be sure to balance out bold colors with neutral accessories. This will help to create a cohesive and polished look.

Second, don't be afraid to mix and match different colors and prints. The 60s were all about experimentation and self-expression, so don't be afraid to take risks with your wardrobe.

Finally, remember that fashion is all about having fun. Don't take yourself too seriously, and don't be afraid to try new things.

Step-by-Step Guide to Creating a 60s-Inspired Outfit

If you're looking to create a 60s-inspired outfit, here's a step-by-step guide:

  1. Start with a bold, bright color like hot pink or yellow.
  2. Add a patterned piece, like a floral skirt or a striped shirt.
  3. Balance out the bold colors and patterns with neutral accessories, like a beige bag or nude heels.
  4. Finish off the look with a pair of oversized sunglasses and a colorful scarf tied around your head.

Tips for Wearing 60s Colors

Here are a few tips for wearing 60s colors:

  • Don't be afraid to mix and match different colors and prints.
  • Balance out bold colors with neutral accessories.
  • Have fun with your wardrobe and don't take yourself too seriously.

FAQs About 60s Colors

Q: What were the most popular colors in the 60s?

A: The most popular colors in the 60s were bright and bold colors like yellow, orange, and hot pink, as well as pastels like baby blue and light pink.

Q: What were the most popular patterns in the 60s?

A: The most popular patterns in the 60s were psychedelic prints, floral prints, and stripes.

Q: How can I incorporate 60s colors into my wardrobe?

A: You can incorporate 60s colors into your wardrobe by wearing bright and bold colors, pastels, neutrals, and psychedelic patterns. Be sure to balance out bold colors with neutral accessories.

Hair Care Tips for a 60s Look

If you're looking to create a 60s-inspired look, your hair is just as important as your outfit. Here are a few hair care tips:

  • Try a bouffant hairstyle for a truly retro look.
  • Use a volumizing shampoo and conditioner to add body to your hair.
  • Invest in a good quality hairspray to keep your hair in place all day.

Conclusion

The 60s were a time of great change in the world, and the colors that people were wearing were no exception. From bright and bold colors to pastels and neutrals, there was something for everyone. Today, you can incorporate 60s colors into your wardrobe in a number of ways, from wearing a bold yellow dress to pairing a floral skirt with a striped shirt. Just remember to have fun with your wardrobe and don't be afraid to take risks.
